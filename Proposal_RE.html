UAS based feature engineering for Barley Yellow Dwarf (BYD) disease prediction in winter wheat

Abstract

Wheat, the major staple crop of the world, is susceptible to many diseases and pests, causing significant yield losses. Timely and precise management of those diseases is therefore necessary to reduce loss and maintain food security. Though Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) have emerged as a revolutionary technology to detect disease symptoms in crops, accurate detection and quantification are still challenging. Thus, this research is conducted to develop models that help in early disease detection in wheat crops using multispectral imagery. For data collection, the multispectral UAVs were flown periodically during the years 2023 and 2024 on wheat breeding trials at the OSU Agronomy Research Station, Stillwater. The flights were carried out at an altitude of 30m with a ground resolution of 1.5cm per pixel, and the ground control points (GCPs) were installed for precise georeferencing. Two sets of vegetation indices (VIs)—three from RGB sensors (VARI, RGBVI, and GRVI) and three from multispectral sensors (NDVI, GNDVI, and NDRE)—will be calculated. Linear regression models will be fitted using i. raw VIs, ii. Statistical descriptors of VIs, and iii. VIs combined with Gabor filters for textural analysis as predictors, whereas the disease ratings will be used as ground truth data. The trained model will be able to show comparable accuracy in disease scoring using UAVs than visual ratings. The developed model could be used to analyze other plant diseases in the future.

Introduction

Wheat, one of the most widely cultivated crops in the world, is affected by various diseases and pests. Diseases such as wheat rust, including stripe rust and leaf rust; fungal diseases such as powdery mildew; and viral diseases such as cereal yellow dwarf and barley yellow dwarf impact the crop heavily, and the quality of products is also decreased. Pests and diseases are blamed for increasing 20 to 40 percent of the food deficit in the world (Sharma, Kooner, and Arora 2017).

Barley Yellow Dwarf (BYD) is one of the major viral diseases caused by multiple viruses from the Luteoviridae family and is transmitted by aphids (Walls, Rajotte, and Rosa 2019). The disease is named Barley Yellow Dwarf, particularly because the symptoms were discovered in Barley for the first time (Rochow 1961), but it does affect many cereal crop species from the Gramineae family. The viruses are transmitted from one plant to another when an aphid or a number of aphids suck the cell sap of an infected plant and inject it into the healthy ones (Abbas 2022). The distinctive symptoms that the plants have when they are infected by the virus are reddening of the flag leaf, shortening of plant height, and low root development (Shah, Bashir, and Manzoor 2012). It significantly reduces plant yield and causes huge losses in production. Several control measures have been used in different parts of the world, including the use of resistant varieties, which is the most prominent and powerful one, along with aphid control and crop rotation (Walls, Rajotte, and Rosa 2019).

The early detection and management of diseases are necessary for loss control, and these are not possible only through the traditional practices such as crop rotation, while a major breakthrough is necessary. Many research studies have been conducted regarding the use of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) for crop monitoring and disease assessment. UAVs are found to provide high-quality images that are free from cloud cover, are of low cost, and have greater flexibility as compared to manned aircraft (Niu et al. 2022). For disease identification, researchers are more focused on integrating Near Infrared (NIR) and Red Edge (RE) bands on them, which helps in detecting diseases such as wheat rust, rice blast, fusarium wilt, and so on (Su et al. 2019).

Different machine learning models are being developed for disease detection in plants such as wheat (Liu et al. 2018), bananas (S. Zhang et al. 2022a), field beans (Wallen 1975), rice (Das et al. 2015), and many more. Traditional machine learning models such as support vector machines (Nema and Dixit 2018) and random forests (Su et al. 2018) are widely used, whereas deep learning methods like Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) (Shelar et al. 2022) and Vision Transformer (VT) (Thai, Le, and Nguyen 2023) are also in practice. Transformer-based models have higher accuracy than CNN, but still have a few limitations, such as computational competency, as they require larger datasets (Guo et al. 2021). Deep learning models, which are a subset of machine learning, are gaining popularity in terms of precision agriculture.

Objectives of research:

  1. To compare vegetation indices derived from RGB and Multispectral sensors for predicting BYD disease
  2. To find out the extent to which pixel-level variation predicts severity
  3. To assess height-based pixel filtering from DEMs to isolate disease-susceptible regions

Literature review

Plant diseases and pests impact crops highly and result in significant yield loss. Being the most common cereal crop in the world, wheat is grown in large areas worldwide. However, growing wheat is challenged by many pathogens, such as viruses, fungi, bacteria, nematodes, and so on, that cause global economic loss of around $200 billion, as per (Q. Zhang et al. 2022). Among different diseases, Barley Yellow Dwarf (BYD) disease, caused by viruses, is found to be the most economically significant viral disease that affects small grains widely (Ali, Hameed, and Tahir 2014).

Barley Yellow Dwarf (BYD) disease

Barley Yellow Dwarf (BYD) disease is a serious cereal crop disease caused by the BYD virus of the Luteoviridae family, though it has more features in common with the Tombusviridae family (Aradottir and Crespo-Herrera 2021), and various studies have been done regarding the classification of BYDV. The most common vectors of BYDV are Rhopalosiphum padi, Rhopalosiphum maidis, Sitobion avenae, Metopolophium dirhodum, Schizaphis graminum, and Sitobion fragariae (Ingwell and Bosque-Pérez 2015). Dwarfing of shoots, discolored leaves (yellow, purple, red, or chlorosis), fewer ears, sterile ears, and delayed heading are all visual symptoms of BYDV (Ali et al. 2018). However, visual screening for BYDV in the field can be deceptive because symptoms tend to be subtle, differ between plant species and types, and trail behind microscopic changes. Additionally, symptoms might be mistaken for other biotic and abiotic stressors (Aradottir and Crespo-Herrera 2021).

Unmanned Aerial Systems (UASs) in Agriculture

Unmanned Aerial Systems (UASs or UAVs), is an aircraft without any human pilot on or inside it, and commonly called as drone. The system includes a ground-based controller, a sensor, and a system of communication with the vehicle (Chang et al. 2017). The flight mission can be remotely operated by a human pilot or with an automated control system using different softwares such as Pix4D mapper, Litchi app, Fly Air, etc. The advancements in technologies in remote sensing has enhanced the data quality and higher spatial as well as temporal data can be collected than conventional manual methods (Bhandari et al. 2023).

Disease Prediction using UAVs

(Zhu et al. 2022b) used partial least squares regression (PLSR), support vector machine regression (SVR), and back propagation neural network (BPNN) as the three algorithms to monitor the wheat scab, among which SVR was found to be the most effective one. (Bhandari et al. 2020) used small unmanned aerial vehicle to assess the foliage disease severity in winter wheat, and both the date of UAV data collection and disease rating taken were nearby. (Su et al. 2018) used five band multispectral camera for the detection of plant stress caused by yellow rust disease in winter wheat. For that, an automated yellow rust detection technique was developed using random forest classifier, by learning from labelled UAV multispectral imagery. (Heidarian Dehkordi et al. 2020) used visual cryptography techniques to differentiate leaf areas diseased by Wheat Stem Rust and Wheat Leaf Rust. For this, UAV were flown at an altitude of 10m with an airspeed of 4m/s, ground sampling distance (GSD) of 0.28 cm, and flights were planned by using Pix4D software. (Bohnenkamp, Behmann, and Mahlein 2019) compared the use of ground as well as UAS sensors for the detection of plant disease using hyper-spectral sensors. The study confirmed that feature selection performs well for the prediction of accuracy if performed in analyzed data set, otherwise fluctuations in data sets impair the accuracy of a data analysis pipeline. (Liu et al. 2018) used UAV for data collection, extracted information from digital images, and correlated image variables with the disease index for the detection of wheat powdery mildew and prediction of grain yield.

Vegetation indices (VIs) based variables

(Huang et al. 2012) adopted vegetation indices (VIs) that were highly sensitive to the changes in plant pigments and stresses, initially selecting 20 distinct VIs and then narrowing the selection to 4 VIs (PRI, PhRI, NPCI and ARI) based on sensitivity to yellow rust disease. However, the results showed that only the PhRI was sensitive to only yellow rust, while the others showed responses to other forms of nutrient stresses as well. The most widely used VI, Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) was used to distinguish vegetation pixels from the background pixels in the research done by (Hao et al. 2025) to detect yellow dwarf disease in wheat. NDVI provided higher statistical dependency as both NIR and Red bands used in NDVI calculation are sensitive to Yellow Rust disease (Su et al. 2019).

Methodology:

Research Site

The research experiment will be conducted at the OSU Agronomy Research Station, located at Stillwater (36°07’19.8’’ N, 97°05’31.5’’ W). The flights will be conducted at an altitude of 30 meters from ground level with a sampling distance of 1.5 cm per pixel. A total of 12 ground control points (GCPs) will be installed for accurate georeferencing of the field.

UAV platform and datasets

The high-resolution multispectral images will be captured by a DJI Phantom 4 multispectral UAV, having six sensors, mounted on a stabilized gimbal. The sensors contain 5 multispectral narrow-band sensors that captures Red, Green, Blue, RedEdge, and NIR bands, respectively, and one RGB sensor. The data from the years 2023-2026 will be used for analysis. Disease ratings of Barley Yellow Dwarf (BYD) will be used as ground truth data. The UAV flights will be in accordance with the dates when the BYD rating will be taken.

Data Processing

To identify which vegetation indices have the highest predicting ability for BYD disease, VIs derived from RGB, multispectral RGB, and multispectral bands—three from each—will be used. The VIs from the RGB bands include VARI, RGBVI, and GRVI, whereas the multispectral bands include NDVI, GNDVI, and NDRE. The multispectral-RGB bands VIs will be similar to that of RGB bands, as only the sensors are different, while the band names are not. The combination of various statistical descriptors (mean, variance, skewness, and B-kurtosis) will be used for spatial analysis.

Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), being the most commonly used VI, was used to distinguish vegetation pixels from the background pixels in the research done by (Hao et al. 2025) to detect yellow dwarf disease in wheat. Similarly, NDVI provided higher statistical dependency, as both NIR and Red bands used in NDVI calculation are sensitive to Yellow Rust disease (Su et al. 2019). Contrast, correlation, homogeneity, energy, entropy, root mean square, skewness, kurtosis, variance, smoothness, etc. are some of the textural features used for plant disease detection (Javidan et al. 2024).

The use of digital elevation models (DEMs) for segregating the pixels with flag leaves on them, as BYD disease is subject to infect flag leaves mostly, could be a new approach that will be used in this research. Height thresholds based on average plot plant height are generated at first from DEM, and later used to subset pixels with height above the thresholds. Three different height thresholds will be considered: 100% of mean height, 80% of mean height, and without using a height threshold.

Plants were rated by counting the number of infected flag leaves as they show the distinct symptoms of BYD, characterized by small reddish to pinkish coloration on leaf tips (Perkins, Steckel, and Stewart 2018). To assess the infection of YD virus in wheat, plants were visually rated by observing the symptoms present on the flag leaves, with no distinguishable symptoms as a 0 rating to a flag leaf completely discolored and curled as a 5 (Bowen et al. 2003).

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) will be used as a dimensionality reduction step to separate several correlated predictors and segregate a new set of eigenvectors to be used as predictors, as related components might bring errors during the model development and prediction. (Wang et al. 2012) used PCA for reducing dimensions in data processing for the image recognition of wheat stripe rust, wheat leaf rust, grape downy mildew, and grape powdery mildew. PCA explains most of the data with minimal loss of information by reducing high dimensional data to a few principal components (D.-Y. Zhang et al. 2020).

The sets of new predictors, generated from PCA, will then be used as predictors in a Bayesian model, whereas the BYD ratings will be used as ground truth data. The model accuracy, precision, macro-precision, and recall will be calculated and interpreted thereafter.

Expected Outcomes

The research is expected to develop a trained model that will show a comparable accuracy in BYD rating using multispectral imagery than visual rating. The use of different raw as well as filtered VIs will detect BYD disease in a more efficient way, and thus developed models, could be used to distinguish other plant diseases in the future. Early detection and management of disease will be possible once the algorithms are developed.

Limitations

  1. Since only the temporal window of BYD disease was considered, no visible patches of BYD were observed in the field during the 2024–25 season, so we could not use the images from that year for our analysis.
  2. It was difficult to align UAV flight schedule with the BYD rating date because of challenging environmental condition for a few days, leading to a slight temporal misalignment between images and ground truth values.
